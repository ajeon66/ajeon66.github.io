Time plots

Data plotted against time - straightforward.

Seasonal plots

Data plotted against seasons in which data was observed . "Seasons" means any recurring period of time in the calendar (seasons, months, hours, etc.).

ggseasonplot(DATA, year.labels=TRUE, year.labels.left=TRUE)

Polar seasonal plot shows data in polar coordinates - just another way of looking at the data.

ggseasonplot(DATA, polar=TRUE)

 

Seasonal subseries plots

Data for each season is consolidated into separate mini time plots. This is basically a "sort by season" visualization - instead of sorting by the time recorded in the data, you sort by season, thus placing the seasons in the x-axis. The blue line indicates the mean for each season.

ggsubseriesplot(DATA)

Trend and seasonality in ACF plots

The autocorrelation values for data with a trend are usually large and positive because observations that occur close to each other are also similar in size. Therefore, as lag lengths get larger and larger, the autocorrelation values decrease.

Additionally, autocorrelation values for data with strong seasonality are larger on the multiples of seasonal lag (e.g. multiples of 4 if there are 4 seasons).

Japan and Australia have had warm relations since the 1970s, with Australia supplying a large amount of Japan's food and raw materials. However the steady decline continuing to the present day requires further research.
      • Australia is also a British Commonwealth Nation, but this doesn't say much about the UK's arrival trends.

      • Steadily increasing trend.
      • Q3 with greatest arrivals, and Q2/Q4 at a close second. Korea-Japan arrivals probably follow a similar trend. Q2, 3, and 4 contain the summer and multiple large holidays, which is when most people travel to Japan from Korea.
